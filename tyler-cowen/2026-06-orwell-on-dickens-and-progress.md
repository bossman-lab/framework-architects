# Orwell on Dickens and Progress

**Date:** June 2026  
**Source:** [Marginal Revolution](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2026/06/orwell-on-dickens-and-progress.html)

Orwell on Dickens: Despite being a "progressive" radical, Dickens is not mechanically minded. He shows no interest in machinery or what machinery can do. As Gissing remarks, Dickens nowhere describes a railway journey with anything like the enthusiasm he shows in describing journeys by stage-coach.

Dickens's novels often feel set in the first quarter of the 19th century. Doyce's "invention" in *Little Dorrit* is represented as extremely ingenious and revolutionary, yet we are never told what the "invention" is.

Dickens shows very little consciousness of the future. When he speaks of human progress it is usually in terms of *moral* progress — men growing better; probably he would never admit that men are only as good as their technical development allows them to be.

He has an infallible moral sense, but very little intellectual curiosity. And here one comes upon something which really is an enormous deficiency in Dickens — that he has no idea of *work*.
