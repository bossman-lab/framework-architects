In August 2024, Dwarkesh Patel interviewed me in person for a text-first conversation about AI scaling, AGI timelines, anonymity, and the work habits behind Gwern.net. Because I did not want audio or video published, the public episode became a re-enactment; this localized version preserves the transcript with links, editorial notes, and background on the production.


The AI discussion centers on the scaling view: intelligence as search over many small programs, deep learning progress as compute plus data plus trial-and-error, and GPT-3 as the decisive public evidence that the connectionist/Moravec-Legg-Kurzweil world had arrived. From that premise follow practical claims about bottom-up corporate automation, human taste as the last scarce input, short AGI planning horizons, writing for LLM training corpora, and the need to record preferences, judgments, and autobiographical detail before they vanish.


The autobiographical half explains the machinery that produced Gwern.net: pseudonymity as a way to be read before being categorized, hearing impairment as a cause of my bookishness and social delay, Wikipedia as apprenticeship into agency, rabbit holes as the unit of motivation, and long essays as accreted pattern-recognition rather than sudden brilliance. It also covers Borges, Gene Wolfe, Ted Chiang, science fiction, site design, Patreon/savings, Bay Area relocation, GLP-1 drugs, psychedelics, AI mind diversity, and the parasocial roles readers project onto a pseudonymous writer.


The through-line is that writing is no longer merely publication. It is agency, memory, and preference-preservation: a way to influence future models, leave recoverable traces for transhuman historians, and define what parts of a human life an AI cannot infer or replace.


In 2024, I agreed to do an interview with podcaster Dwarkesh Patel about AI scaling laws and myself. Below is a localized and further annotated version of the original Dwarkesh post.

# Background


I had met him at a private conference and liked him. We had been chatting for a while, and as he interviewed more and more people related to AI scaling, I felt that I wanted to do an interview to get my small part of the story on the record (these interviews became The Scaling Era: An Oral History of AI, 2019–62025), help out a podcaster I respected for doing the best work around (better than more famous AI interview podcasters), and satisfy persistent demands that I “go on a podcast”.


My one condition was that I didn’t want video/audio published, only a text transcript, which I could eventually mirror on Gwern.net—for general reasons of privacy like past attacks on myself or my family, and long-term trends in cybersecurity, but he could ask about any topic he wanted, including growing up hearing-impaired.


Dwarkesh prepared thoroughly for his interview, and in August 2024, on a trip to the Bay Area, I spent several hours talking with him. (I was a little shocked that it came in at >15,000 words.) We prepared a transcript, rearranging some questions, removing padding, and adding some useful links.


He asked to do a video anyway because people enjoy videos (apparently, there is almost no overlap between people who will watch a Dwarkesh Patel podcast and people who will read a transcript). They went through a number of iterations, where some voice actors or CGI services or voice synthesis attempts didn’t work out, and eventually settled on an interesting strategy: Dwarkesh would re-enact the interview with his friend Chris Painter, for better chemistry, and then replace Painter with an avatar.


I thought this was hilarious, but was unsure if this was a good idea; Dwarkesh went ahead with it, and his viewers seemed to all love it and the episode did very well. (I kept asking people who mentioned seeing it, and they were uniformly positive, although when some clips were reposted in May 2026 to Twitter, most of the comments extremely negative, so perhaps this has changed.) Amusingly, a fair number of people criticized the ‘AI voice’ for being unconvincing and robotic; I occasionally mentioned this to Painter, who responded by making a “I AM NOT GWERN”+logo t-shirt to wear.


In particular, the call for donations at the end did far better than I expected. I received six figures of donations within the next few weeks and many offers of short-term housing, and so I sped up moving to the Bay Area, and started spending much more time there in May 2025.


# Anonymous Writer Who Predicted AI Trajectory On $1​2K⧸year Salary; Legacy & Anonymity In The Age Of AGI


Gwern is a pseudonymous researcher and writer. He was one of the first people to see LLM scaling coming. If you’ve read his blog, you know he’s one of the most interesting polymathic thinkers alive.


In order to protect Gwern’s anonymity, I proposed interviewing him in person, and having my friend Chris Painter voice over his words after. This amused him enough that he agreed.


After the episode, I convinced Gwern to create a donation page where people can help sustain what he’s up to. Please go here to contribute.


# Anonymity


- 

Dwarkesh Patel: Today I’m interviewing Gwern Branwen. Gwern is an anonymous researcher and writer. He’s deeply influenced the people building AGI. He was one of the first people to see LLM scaling coming. If you’ve read his blog, you’ll know he’s one of the most interesting polymathic thinkers alive. We recorded this conversation in person. In order to protect Gwern’s anonymity, we created this avatar. This isn’t his voice. This isn’t his face. But these are his words.


What is the most underrated benefit of anonymity?
- 

Gwern Branwen: The most underrated benefit of anonymity is that people don’t project onto you as much. They can’t slot you into any particular niche or identity and write you off in advance. They have to at least read you a little bit to even begin to dismiss you.


It’s great that people cannot retaliate against you. I have derived a lot of benefit from people not being able to mail heroin to my home and call the police to SWAT me. But I always feel that the biggest benefit is just that you get a hearing at all. You don’t get immediately written off by the context.


# Automating Steve Jobs


- 

D: Do you expect companies to be automated top-down (starting with the CEO) or bottom-up (starting with all the workers)?
- 

G: All the pressures are to go bottom-up. From existing things, it’s just much more palatable in every way to start at the bottom and replace there and work your way up, to eventually where you just have human executives overseeing a firm of AIs.


Also from a RL perspective, if we are in fact better than AIs in some way, it should be in the long-term vision thing. The AI will be too myopic to execute any kind of novel long-term strategy and seize new opportunities.


​ That would presumably give you this paradigm where you have a human CEO who does the vision thing. And then the AI corporation scurries around doing his bidding. They don’t have the taste that the CEO has. You have one Steve Jobs-type at the helm, and then maybe a whole pyramid of AIs out there executing it and bringing him new proposals. He looks at every individual thing and says, “No, that proposal is bad. This one is good.”


That may be hard to quantify, but the human-led firms should, under this view, then outcompete the entirely AI firms, which would keep making myopic ‘justifiable’ choices that just don’t quite work out in the long term.


I think, despite the massive investment in RL over the past 3 years, which have produced the expected capability gains, this is what we have seen: a lack of taste. The most characteristic flaw of my LLM coding or writing these days is not any outright error or confabulation, but it is just missing a magic spark of creativity.


Even when the choices are fine seen once, they suffer from mode-collapse. This is one reason I don’t get much out of Claude for creative writing; it is mode-collapsed on the idea level, circling back to the same points. No matter how much material I give it, like my enormous “Manual of Style” to “how to write like Gwern”, it’s still dull. Bland and inoffensive.


I keep having to curate the good sample, or come up with the original idea. So I expect LLMs to keep getting longer time-horizons on things like the famous METR graph for well-specified tasks, but for LLMs to keep leaning on Steve Jobs types for a specification worth specifying at all.


Simply maximizing quarterly returns for corporation may eventually brute-force it—after all, “reward is enough”—but a clock ticking only a few times a year is not the fast tempo RL training thrives at…


- 

D: What is the last thing you’d be personally doing? What is the last keystroke that gets automated for you?
- 

G: The last thing that I see myself still doing right before the nanobots start eating me from the bottom up and I start screaming, “No, I specifically requested the opposite of this…” Right before that, I think what I’m still doing is the Steve Jobs-thing of choosing. My AI minions are bringing me wonderful essays. I’m saying, “This one is better. This is the one that I like”, and possibly building on that and saying, “That’s almost right, but you know what would make it really good? If you pushed it to 11 in this way.”


- 

D: If we do have firms that are made up of AIs, what do you expect the unit of selection to be? Will it be individual models? Will it be the firm as a whole? With humans, we have these debates about whether it’s kin-level selection, individual-level selection, or gene-level selection. What will it be for the AIs?
- 

G: Once you can replicate individual models perfectly, the unit of selection can move way up and you can do much larger groups and packages of minds. That would be an obvious place to start. You can train individual minds in a differentiable way, but then you can’t really train the interaction between them. You will have groups of models or minds of people who just work together really well in a global sense, even if you can’t attribute it to any particular aspect of their interactions. There are some places you go and people just work well together. There’s nothing specific about it, but for whatever reason they all just click in just the right way.


That seems like the most obvious unit of selection. You would have packages—I guess possibly department units—where you have a programmer and a manager type, then you have maybe a secretary type, maybe a financial type, a legal type. This is the default package where you just copy everywhere you need a new unit. At this level, you can start evolving them and making random variations to each and then keep the one that performs best.


# Isaac Newton’s Theory Of Progress


- 

D: By when could one have foreseen the Singularity? Obviously, Moravec and others are talking about it in the 1980s and 1990s. You could have done it decades earlier. When was the earliest you could have seen where things were headed?
- 

G: If you want to trace the genealogy there, you’d have to at least go back as far as Samuel Butler’s Erewhon in 1872154ya or his essay before that. In 1863163ya, he describes explicitly his vision of a machine life becoming ever more developed until eventually it’s autonomous. At which point, that’s a threat to the human race. This is why he concluded, “war to the death should be instantly proclaimed against them.” That’s prescient for 1863163ya! I’m not sure that anyone has given a clear Singularity scenario earlier than that. The idea of technological progress was still relatively new at that point.


I love the example of Isaac Newton looking at the rates of progress in Newton’s time and going, “Wow, there’s something strange here! Stuff is being invented now. We’re making progress. How is that possible?” And then coming up with the answer, “Well, progress is possible now because civilization gets destroyed every couple of thousand years, and all we’re doing is we’re rediscovering the old stuff.”


That’s Newton’s explanation for technological acceleration! “We can’t actually have any kind of real technological acceleration. It must be because the world gets destroyed periodically and we just can’t see past the last reset.”
- 

D: It’s almost like Fermi’s paradox, but for different civilizations across time with respect to each other instead of aliens across space.
- 

G: Yeah. It turns out even Lucretius, around 1,700 years before that, is writing the same argument.


“Look at all these wonderful innovations and arts and sciences that we Romans have compiled together in the Roman empire! This is amazing, but it can’t actually be a recent acceleration in technology. Could that be real? …No, that’s crazy. Obviously, the world was recently destroyed.”
- 

D: Interesting.
- 

G: It is, it is.


# Grand Theory Of Intelligence


- 

D: What is the grand parsimonious theory of intelligence going to look like? It seems like you have all of these trends across different fields—like scaling laws in AI, like the scaling of the human brain when we went from primates to humans, the uniformity of the neocortex—and basically many other things which seem to be pointing towards some grand theory that should exist which explains what intelligence is. What do you think that will look like?
- 

G: ​ The 10,000 foot view of intelligence, that I think the success of scaling points to, is that all intelligence is, is search over Turing machines. Anything that happens can be described by Turing machines of various lengths. All we are doing when we are doing “learning”, or when we are doing “scaling”, is that we’re searching over more and longer Turing machines, and we are applying them in each specific case.


Otherwise, there is no general master algorithm. There is no special intelligence fluid. It’s just a tremendous number of special cases that we learn and we encode into our brains.


- 

D: I don’t know. When I look at the ways in which my smart friends are smart, it just feels more like a general horsepower kind of thing. They’ve just got more juice. That seems more compatible with this master algorithm perspective rather than this Turing machine perspective. It doesn’t really feel like they’ve got this long tail of Turing machines that they’ve learned. How does this picture account for variation in human intelligence?
- 

G: No IQ gland. Well, yeah. When we talk about more or less intelligence, it’s just that they have more compute in order to do search over more Turing machines for longer.


[An example I’d give now is: new GPUs are much faster than older GPUs. They train far smarter artificial neural networks. But is there some “master algorithm” that a new GPU knows and an old GPU lacks? Is there some special ‘intelligence’ when a new one multiplies two FP32 numbers in somehow a superior way? Or is it just that it can do multiplications faster on more numbers?


It is the latter, and we can even run the final fully trained neural networks on the old GPUs and get the exact same results, if we can hook them up correctly and wait a while.]


I don’t think there’s anything else other than that. So from any learned brain you could extract small solutions to specific problems, because all the large brain is doing with the compute is finding it.


That’s why you never find any “IQ gland”. There is nowhere in the brain where, if you hit it, you eliminate fluid intelligence. This doesn’t exist. Because what your brain is doing is a lot of learning of individual specialized problems. Once those individual problems are learned, then they get recombined for fluid intelligence. And that’s just, you know… intelligence.


Typically with a large neural network model, you can always pull out a small model which does a specific task equally well. Because that’s all the large model is. It’s just a gigantic ensemble of small models tailored to the ever-escalating number of tiny problems you have been feeding them.


[This should make the continual success of ever smaller LLMs less surprising.]


- 

D: If intelligence is just search over Turing machines—and of course intelligence is tremendously valuable and useful—doesn’t that make it more surprising that intelligence took this long to evolve in humans?
- 

G: Intelligence rarely pays. Not really, I would actually say that it helps explain why human-level intelligence is not such a great idea and so rare to evolve. Because any small Turing machine could always be encoded more directly by your genes, with sufficient evolution. You have these organisms where their entire neural network is just hard-coded by the genes. So if you could do that, obviously that’s way better than some sort of colossally expensive, unreliable, glitchy search process—like what humans implement—which takes whole days, in some cases, to learn. Whereas you could be hardwired right from birth.


For many creatures, it just doesn’t pay to be intelligent because that’s not actually adaptive. There are better ways to solve the problem than a general purpose intelligence.


In any kind of niche where it’s static, or where intelligence will be super expensive, or where you don’t have much time because you’re a short-lived organism, it’s going to be hard to evolve a general purpose learning mechanism when you could instead evolve one that’s tailored to the specific problem that you encounter.


# Seeing Scaling Early


- 

D: You’re one of the only people outside OpenAI in 2020 who had a picture of the way in which AI was progressing and had a very detailed theory, an empirical theory of scaling in particular.


I’m curious what processes you were using at the time which allowed you to see the picture you painted in the “Scaling Hypothesis” post that you wrote at the time.
- 

G: If I had to give an intellectual history of that for me, it would start in the mid-2000s when I’m reading Moravec and Ray Kurzweil. At the time, they’re making this kind of fundamental connectionist argument that if you had enough computing power, that could result in discovering the neural network architecture that matches the human brain. And that until that happens, until that amount of computing power is available, AI is basically futile.


To me, I found this argument very unlikely, because it’s very much a “build it and they will come” view of progress, which at the time I just did not think was correct. I thought it was ludicrous to suggest that simply because there’s some supercomputer out there which matches the human brain, then that would just summon out of nonexistence the correct algorithm.


Algorithms are really complex and hard! They require deep insight—or at least I thought they did. It seemed like really difficult mathematics. You can’t just buy a bunch of computers and expect to get this advanced AI out of it! It just seemed like magical thinking.


So I knew the argument, but I was super skeptical. I didn’t pay too much attention, but Shane Legg and some others were very big on this in the years following. And as part of my interest in transhumanism and LessWrong and AI risk, I was paying close attention to Legg’s blog posts where he’s extrapolating out the trend with updated numbers from Kurzweil and Moravec. And he’s giving very precise predictions about how “we’re going to get the first generalist system around 2019, as Moore’s law keeps going. And then around 2025, we’ll get the first human-ish agents with generalist capabilities. Then by 2030, we should have AGI.”


Along the way, DanNet and AlexNet came out. When those came out I was like, “Wow, that’s a very impressive success story of connectionism. But is it just an isolated success story? Or is this what Kurzweil and Moravec and Legg were predicting— that we would get GPUs and then better algorithms would just show up?”


So I started thinking to myself that this is something to keep an eye on. Maybe this is not quite as stupid an idea as I had originally thought. I just keep reading deep learning literature and noticing again and again that the dataset size keeps getting bigger. The models keep getting bigger. The GPUs slowly crept up from one GPU—the cheapest consumer GPU—to two, and then they were eventually training on 8.


And you can just see the fact that the neural networks keep expanding from these incredibly niche use cases that do next to nothing. The use just kept getting broader and broader and broader. I would say to myself, “Wow, is there anything CNNs can’t do‽” I would just see people apply CNN to something else every individual day on arXiv.


So for me it was this gradual trickle of drops hitting me in the background as I was going along with my life. Every few days, another drop would fall. I’d go, “Huh? Maybe intelligence really is just a lot of compute applied to a lot of data, applied to a lot of parameters. Maybe Moravec and Legg and Kurzweil were right.” I’d just note that, and continue on, thinking to myself, “Huh, if that was true, it would have a lot of implications.”


So there was no real eureka moment there. It was just continually watching this trend that no one else seemed to see, except possibly a handful of people like Ilya Sutskever, or Schmidhuber. I would just pay attention and notice that the world over time looked more like their world than it looked like my world—where algorithms are super important and you need like deep insight to do stuff. Their world just kept happening.


And then GPT-1 comes out and I was like, “Wow, this unsupervised sentiment neuron is just learning on its own. That’s pretty amazing.” It was also a very compute-centric view. You just build the Transformer and the intelligence will come.


And then GPT-2 comes out and I had this “holy s⁠—t!” moment. You look at the prompting and the summarization [in the GPT-2 paper]: “Holy s⁠—t, do we live in their world?”


And then GPT-3 comes out and that was the crucial test. It’s a big, big scale-up. It’s one of the biggest scale-ups in all neural network history. Going from GPT-2 to GPT-3, that’s not a super narrow specific task like Go. It really seemed like it was the crucial test. If scaling was bogus, then the GPT-3 paper should just be unimpressive and wouldn’t show anything important. Whereas if scaling was true, you would just automatically be guaranteed to get so much more impressive results out of it than GPT-2.


I opened up the first page, maybe the second page, and I saw the few-shot learning chart. And I’m like, “Holy s⁠—t, we are living in the scaling world. Legg and Moravec and Kurzweil were right!”


And then I turned to Twitter and everyone else was like, “Oh, you know, this shows that scaling works so badly. Why, it’s not even state-of-the-art!” That made me so angry I had to write all this up. Someone was wrong on the Internet.


- 

D: I remember in 2020, people were writing bestselling books about AI. It was definitely a thing people were talking about, but people were not noticing the most salient things in retrospect: LLMs, GPT-3, scaling laws. All these people who are talking about AI but missing this crucial crux, what were they getting wrong?
- 

G: I think for the most part they were suffering from two issues.


First, they had not been paying attention to all of the scaling results before that which were relevant. They had not really appreciated the fact that, for example, AlphaZero was discovered in part by DeepMind doing Bayesian optimization on the hyperparameters and noticing that you could just get rid of more and more of the Monte Carlo tree search as you went and you got better models. That was a critical insight, which could only have been gained by having so much compute power that you could afford to train many, many versions and see the difference that that made.


Similarly, those people simply did not know about the Baidu paper on scaling laws in 2017, which showed that the scaling laws just keep going and going forever, practically. It should have been the most important paper of the year, but a lot of people just did not prioritize it. It didn’t have any immediate implication, and so it sort of got forgotten. People were too busy discussing Transformers or AlphaZero or something to really notice it.


So that was one issue. Another issue is that they shared the basic error I was making about algorithms being more important than compute. This was, in part, due to a systematic falsification of the actual origins of ideas in the research literature. Papers do not tell you where the ideas come from in a truthful manner. They just tell you a nice sounding story about how it was discovered. They don’t tell you how it’s actually discovered.


So even if you appreciate the role trial and error [eg. in inventing resnets] and compute power in your own experiment as a researcher, you probably just think, “Oh, I got lucky that way. My experience is unrepresentative. Over in the next lab, there they do things by the power of thought and deep insight.”


Then it turns out that everywhere you go, compute and data, trial and error, and serendipity play enormous roles in how things actually happened. Once you understand that, then you understand why compute comes first. You can’t do trial and error and serendipity without it. You can write down all these beautiful ideas, but you just can’t test them out.


Even a small difference in hyperparameters, or a small choice of architecture, can make a huge difference to the results. When you only can do a few instances, you would typically find that it doesn’t work, and you would give up and you would go away and do something else.


Whereas if you had more compute power, you could keep trying. Eventually, you hit something that works great. Once you have a working solution, you can simplify it and improve it and figure out why it worked and get a nice, robust solution that would work no matter what you did to it. But until then, you’re stuck. You’re just flailing around in this regime where nothing works.


So you have this horrible experience going through the old deep learning literature and seeing all sorts of contemporary ideas people had back then, which were completely correct. But they didn’t have the compute to train what you know would have worked. It’s just tremendously tragic. You can look at things like ResNets being published back in 1988, instead of 201511ya.


And it would have worked! It did work, but at such a small scale that it was irrelevant. You couldn’t use it for anything real. It just got forgotten, so you had to wait until 201511ya for ResNets to actually come along and be a revolution in deep learning.


So that’s kind of the double bias of why you would believe that scaling was not going to work. You did not notice the results that were key, in retrospect, like the BigGAN scaling to 300 million images. There are still people today who would tell you with a straight face that GANs cannot scale past millions of images. They just don’t know that BigGAN handled 300 million images without a sweat. If you don’t know that, well you probably would easily think, “Oh, GANs are broken.” But if you do know that, then you think to yourself, “How can algorithms be so important when all these different generative architectures all work so well—as long as you have lots and lots of GPUs?” That’s the common ingredient. You have to have lots and lots of GPUs.


# AGI Timelines


- 

D: What do your timelines look like over the last 20 years? Is AI just monotonically getting closer over time?
- 

G: ~2 years⧸year. I would say it was very far away, from like 2005–5201016ya. It was somewhere well past like 2050. It was close enough that I thought I might live to see it, but I was not actually sure if there was any reasonable chance.


But once AlexNet and DanNet came out, then it just kept dropping at a rate of like 2 years per year, every year until now. We just kept on hitting barriers to deep learning and doing better. Regardless of how it was doing it, it was obviously getting way better. It just seemed none of the alternative paradigms were doing well. This one was doing super well.


- 

D: Was there a time that you felt you had updated too far?
- 

G: AlphaGo over-update. Yeah, there were a few times I thought I had overshot. I thought people over-updated on AlphaGo. They went too far on AI hype with AlphaGo. Afterwards, when pushes into big reinforcement learning efforts kind of all fizzled out—like post-Dota, as the reinforcement learning wasn’t working out for solving those hard problems outside of the simulated game universes⁠—then I started thinking, “Okay, maybe we kinda overshot there…”


But then GPT came out of nowhere and basically erased all that. It was like, “Oh, s⁠—t. Here’s how RL is going to work. It’s going to be the cherry on the cake. We’re just going to focus on the cake for a while.” Now we have actually figured out a good recipe for baking a cake, which was not true before.


Before, it seemed like you were going to have to brute-force it end-to-end from the rewards [because at least in theory, “reward is enough”]. But now you can do the LeCun thing, of learning fast on generative models and then just doing a little bit of RL on top to make it do something specific.


# What To Do In Remaining 3 Years Until AGI


- 

D: Now that you know that AGI is a thing that’s coming, what’s your thinking around how you see your role in this timeline? How are you thinking about how to spend these next few years?
- 

G: I have been thinking about that quite a lot. What do I want to do? What would be useful to do?


I’m doing things now because I want to do them, regardless of whether it will be possible for an AI to do them in like 3 years. I do something because I want to. Because I like it, I find it funny or whatever. Or I think carefully about doing just the human part of it, like laying out a proposal for something.


If you take seriously the idea of getting AGI in a few years, you don’t necessarily have to implement stuff and do it yourself. You can sketch out clearly what you want, and why it would be good and how to do it. And then just wait for the better AGI to come along and actually do it then. Unless there’s some really compelling reason to do it right now and pay that cost of your scarce time.


[cf. “Write More Online”], “Writing for the LLMs”


But otherwise, I’m trying to write more about what is not recorded. Things like preferences and desires and evaluations and judgments. Things that an AI could not replace even in principle.


​ The way I like to put it is that “the AI cannot eat ice cream for you”. It cannot decide for you which kind of ice cream you like. Only you can do that. And if anything else did, it would be worthless, because it’s not your particular preference.


That’s kind of the rubric. Is this something I want to do regardless of any future AI, because I enjoy it? Or is this something where I’m doing only the human part of it and the AGI can later on do it? Or is this writing down something that is unwritten and thus helping the future AI versions of me?


So if it doesn’t fall under those 3, I have been trying to not do it.

I liked to read The New Republic and National Review, and Time and Newsweek and U.S. News & World Report, and Electronic Gaming Monthly vs Official U.S. PlayStation Magazine vs Nintendo Power, back to back for the perspective. (Unfortunately, my high school library was too cheap to spring for The Economist as well, and I had to get copies from my uncle. I do not remember if they had The New Yorker or if I started reading that online.) Since the selection of magazines never changed and I had many free periods to kill, I wound up reading essentially every issue each periodical published for 4 years, which was enough depth to see many things in each one. So it was an education in media literacy, although at the time I wished for much more diversity.


It was educational to compare so many different takes on the same events, often when a substantial amount of news had happened since, because the selected context for each opinion could be so different, and omissions so glaring. Two articles or op-eds on the same topic could cover such different parts of the proverbial elephant; you did indeed have to read everything if you wanted to know what was going on, and this was an attitude I took with me into my WP editing and later essays. Reading ideologues and partisans at work in real time was valuable for showing me how flexible political beliefs could be—especially when Obama was elected and one could see how things like drone warfare or the NSA suddenly became acceptable on the left. My reading did not leave me particularly impressed with either Democrats or Republicans as a whole, but at least in that era, the Republicans had more of a monopoly on stupid ideas like “invade Iraq”. It was also educational to see how shamelessly and blatantly dishonest a non-political periodical like Nintendo Power could be (apparently Nintendo never made a bad game), or how video game journalism in general struggled to be independent at all rather than company propaganda outlets. (For the same reason, I am distrustful of any one LLM’s output. I would much rather see several compared, or at least reviewing each other. If you can’t turn every page on a topic and penetrate at least the first layer of discourse (eg. spotting the introductory round of omissions or misstatements in the argument tree), then perhaps you have an obligation to remain ignorant.


And if I had read all the worthwhile magazines, I might read The New York Times. I didn’t make a point of trying to read newspapers beyond my local daily newspaper, though; I didn’t like the repetition of daily news when I could get a better perspective from the weekly magazines. (I think weekly is the best tempo for general news: daily is repetitive, but the monthly ones tended to be hopelessly out of date yet confidently wrong, and thus actively misleading and redundant with what I might’ve read long ago in the others or in blogs/online news, and surprisingly difficult to read as one keeps having to re-interpret what authors wrote to allow for their ignorance at the unspecified time they were writing their piece before the formal publication date, which meant that a freshly-arrived piece published “yesterday” might be several months out of date in a specific assertion—particularly during elections.) While some of the weekly magazines like The Economist are still healthy and even publishing print editions, probably these days you’d have to find good newsletters to supplement them.


Ever since I have been wary of reading too much daily news; there’s a risk in not reading it at all, in working with “your office door closed” to paraphrase Hamming, but there’s also a risk of crowding out meaningful work in favor of junk food news. (As I write this, almost every time I have visited the New York Times homepage for the past 2 months, the top news item has been some variation of “a peace deal with Iran is imminent” or “a peace deal has fallen through”; fortunately, I was not there for this pseudo-news but more interesting longform articles like a profile of AISI.) I’d say, looking around me, there are many more people who overconsume daily news than underconsume it. It seems like reading news makes people feel informed in the way that voting makes them feel powerful or playing the lottery makes them feel rich or cleaning makes them feel productive. But you are not a newsletter writer like Matt Levine, nor are you a professional pollster like Nate Silver; you do not actually need to have an opinion on most events. And you should shun social media which functions in a similar way. (For example, shortform video sites are even worse in some ways than daily newspapers, while link aggregators like Hacker News are underestimated for how “evergreen” much of their content is, and the implicit signal of opening doors to see what people are working on and where things are trending, in ways that may not be reported on for a long time, if ever. I think link aggregators are not as much of a waste of time as they seem, especially if they help you find niche voices to follow.)


So my advice to kids would be to binge on daily news for a few years, to watch the issue-attention tide surge and ebb. Ideally, do this by sitting down and reading through a stack of daily newspapers. (We could imagine an improved AI version of this which simply clustered articles on each topic and visualized the key differences.) But whatever you pick, binge them so you can see the endless churn and meaninglessness of most of it while the vast importance of the remaining things which do matter, how often the initial reports are nonsense or fatally incomplete (truly, “one story is good until another is told”), and start to see the threadbare set of tropes and narratives that power journalism, and how many important things were recognized all too late and how journalism was not the “first draft of history”. Once you know it’s not, you can treat it with its due weight, with its biases like focuses on people and gossip and acute events, and neglecting important things like slow trends. Once you have learned everything useful, and you’ve binged until you’ve become sick and developed allergies to the daily news, then you can cut back to the right amount of news for you, which may be little or none, with a better intuition for what will matter.


(I wonder when the first major media article on GPT-3 was…? Or the first one that took seriously the idea that we now lived in the scaling world, rather than pattern-matching to “the AI bubble” or “stochastic parrots”?)

So from that perspective, it’s miraculous that you can ask an LLM to try to do all these things and they have a non-trivial success rate. If you told people 10 years ago—that you could just behavior-clone on individual letters following one by one, and you could get coherent action out of it and control robots and write entire programs⁠—their jaws would drop and they would say that you’ve been huffing too many fumes from DeepMind or something.


The reason that agency doesn’t work is that we do so little actual agency training at all. An example of how you would do agency directly would be like Gato from DeepMind. There they’re actually training agents. Instead we train them on Internet scrapes which merely encode the outputs of agents or occasional descriptions of agents doing things. There’s no actual logging of state/action/result/reward sequences like a proper reinforcement learning setup would have.


I would say that what’s more interesting is that nobody wants to train agents in a proper reinforcement learning way. Instead, everyone wants to train LLMs and do everything with as little RL as possible in the backend.


[Since this interview, labs have started real RL training, and LLM capabilities still rely on powerful pretrained base models to elicit from using RL. See Jones 2021. This is because RL is one of the most expensive possible ways to learn anything.]


# Borges & Literature


- 

D: What would a person like you be doing before the Internet existed?
- 

G: If the Internet did not exist, I would have to have tried to make it in regular academia and maybe narrow my interests a lot more, something I could publish on regularly.


Or I could possibly have tried to opt out and become a librarian like one of my favorite writers, Jorge Luis Borges. He was a librarian until he succeeded as a writer. Of course, I’ve always agreed with him about imagining paradise as a kind of library. I love libraries.


I regret that all the reading I do is now on the computer and I don’t get to spend much time in physical libraries. I do genuinely love them, just poring through the stacks and looking for random stuff. Some of the best times for me in university was being able to go through these gigantic stacks of all sorts of obscure books and just looking at a random spine, pulling stuff off the shelf and reading obscure, old technical journals to see all the strange and wonderful things they were doing back then, which now have been forgotten.


[This also proved to be surprisingly helpful for understanding AI scaling, because I had read a lot of symbolic AI papers and journals while researching Lisp machines and so had a good perspective on what an “AI bubble” looks like… and what it doesn’t look like, and how incredibly limited previous AI systems had been. So when DL scaling started to work, I could see how different it was this time.]


- 

D: If you could ask Borges one question, what would it be?
- 

G: Oh. He’s a real hero of mine. This is not something I want to give a bad answer to.


[“Would it have been worth living if you could never write, only read, like the people in ‘The Library of Babel’?”]
- 

D: Can I ask why he’s a hero of yours?
- 

G: When I was younger, one of the science fiction books that really impressed me was Dan Simmons’s Hyperion, especially The Fall of Hyperion. In there, he alludes to Kevin Kelly’s Out of Control book, which strongly features the parable of “The Library of Babel”. From there, I got the collected editions of Borges’ fiction and nonfiction. I just read through them again and again.


I was blown away by the fact that you could be so creative, with all this polymathic knowledge and erudition, and write these wonderful, entertaining, provocative short stories and essays. [The essays are underrated.] I thought to myself, “If I could be like any writer—any writer at all—I would not mind being Borges.”
- 

D: Borges has a short poem called “Borges and I” where he talks about how he doesn’t identify with the version of himself that is actually doing the writing and publishing all of this great work. I don’t know if you identify with that at all.
- 

G: When I was a kid, I did not understand that essay, but I think I understand it now.


- 

D: What are other pieces of other literature that you encountered where now you really understand what they were getting at but you didn’t when you first came across them?
- 

G: Ted Chiang’s “Story of Your Life”. I completely blew it understanding it the first time I read it. I had to get a lot more context where I could actually go back and understand what his point was. Gene Wolfe’s “Suzanne Delage” story was a complete mystery to me. It took like 14 years to actually understand it. But I’m very proud of that one.
- 

D: What did you figure out about “Suzanne Delage”?
- 

G: ​ Gene Wolfe’s “Suzanne Delage” is a very, very short story about a guy remembering not meeting a woman in his local town and thinking, “Oh, that’s kind of strange.” That’s the whole story. Nobody has any idea what it means, even though we’re told that it means something. Gene Wolfe is a genius writer, but nobody could figure it out for like 40 years.


Last year I figured it out. It turns out it’s actually a subtle retelling of Dracula, where Dracula invades the town and steals the woman from him. He’s been brainwashed by Dracula—in a very Bram Stoker way—to forget it all. Every single part of the story is told by what’s not said in the narrator’s recollection. It’s incredible. It’s the only story I know which is so convincingly written by what’s not in it.


- 

D: That’s crazy that you figured that out. The Ted Chiang story, the “Story of Your Life”, can you remind me what that one’s about?
- 

G: The surface story is just about a bunch of weird aliens who came to Earth.
- 

D: Oh, that’s right, yeah. It’s the same plot as Arrival.
- 

G: They had a weird language which didn’t have a sense of time. The narrator learned to see the future, and then the aliens left.
- 

D: What is it that you realized about that story?
- 

G: The first time I read it, it struck me as just a kind of stupid ESP story about seeing the future, very stupid, boring, standard conventional, verbose, and dragging in much irrelevant physics. Only a while after that did I understand that it was not about time travel or being able to see the future.


It was instead about a totally alien kind of mind that’s equally valid in its own way, in which you see everything as part of an already determined story heading to a predestined end. This turned out to be mathematically equivalent and equally powerful as our conventional view of the world—events marching one by one to an unknown and changing future.


That was a case where Chiang was just writing at too high a level for me to understand. I pattern-matched it to some much more common, stupid story.


- 

D: How do you think about the value of reading fiction versus nonfiction?
- 

G: You could definitely spend the rest of your life reading fiction and not benefit whatsoever from it other than having memorized a lot of trivia about things that people made up.


I tend to be pretty cynical about the benefits of fiction. Most fiction is not written to make you better in any way. It’s written just to entertain you, or to exist and to fill up time.
- 

D: ​ But it sounds like your own ideas have benefited a lot from the sci-fi that you read.
- 

G: Yeah, but it’s extremely little sci-fi. Easily 99% of the sci-fi I read was completely useless to me. I could have easily cut it down to 20 novels or short stories which actually were good enough and insightful enough to actually change my view. One volume of Blindsight by Peter Watts is worth all 100+ Xanth novels, or all 500 Expanded Universe novels of Star Wars.
- 

D: The ones that you did find insightful, the top 20 or so, what did they have in common?
- 

G: I would say that the characteristic they have is taking non-human intelligence seriously.


It doesn’t have to be artificial intelligence necessarily. It’s taking the idea of non-human intelligence seriously and not imagining your classic sci-fi scenario of humans going out into the galaxy with ray guns—the sort of thing where you have rockets and ray guns but you don’t have cell phones.


People complain that the Singularity is a sort of boring, overused sci-fi trope. But if you went out and actually grabbed random books of science fiction, you would find that less than 1% contain anything remotely like that, or have any kind of relevance to the current context that we actually face with AI.


[Another way to put it is that Singularity works like A Fire Upon the Deep or Pantheon seem exhausting because there are almost no SF works that seriously deal with AI in a way which now reads as anything but quaint, and so they are over-exposed.


Unfortunately, as of May 2026, we can still list the SF authors worth reading on one hand.]

# Gwern’s Intelligence And Process


- 

D: Do people tend to underestimate or overestimate your intelligence?
- 

G: I would say they overestimate it. They mistake for intelligence the fact that I remember many things, that I have written many things over many years. They imagine that if they sat me down, I could do it all spontaneously at the moment that they’re talking to me. But with many things I have thought about, I have the advantage of having looked at things before. So I’m cheating. When I talk to people, I may just be quoting something I’ve already written, or at least thought about.


So I come off as a lot smarter than I actually am. I would say I’m not really all that smart, compared to many people I’ve known, who update very fast on the fly. But in the end, it’s the output that matters, right?
- 

D: I guess there is an on-the-fly intelligence. But there’s another kind too which is this ability to synthesize things over a long period of time, and then come up with grand theories as a result of these different things that you’re seeing. I don’t think that’s just crystallized intelligence, right?
- 

G: It’s not just crystallized intelligence, but if you could see all the individual steps in my process, you’d be a lot less impressed. If you could see all of the times I just note down something like, “Hmm, that’s funny.” Or, “Huh, another example of that”, and if you just saw each particular step, you would say that what I was doing was reasonable and not some huge sign of brilliance. It would make sense to you in that moment. It’s only when that happens over a decade, and you don’t see the individual stuff, that my output at the end looks like magic.


One of my favorite quotes about this process is from the magicians Penn & Teller. Teller says “magic is putting in more effort than any reasonable person would expect you to.” He tells a story about how they make cockroaches appear from a top hat. The trick is that they researched and found special cockroaches, and then found special styrofoam to trap the cockroaches, and arranged all that, for just a single trick. No reasonable person would do that, but they did because they wanted the trick to really pay off. The result is cockroaches somehow appearing from an empty hat.


If you could see each step, it would make sense on its own, it would just look effortful. But when you see only the final trick, then that whole process and its output becomes magic.


- 

D: That’s one of the interesting things about your process. There are a couple of writers like Matt Levine or Byrne Hobart who write an article every day. I think of them almost like autoregressive models. For you, on some of the blog posts you can see the start date and end date that you list on your website of when you’ve been working on a piece. Sometimes it’s like 2009–152024. I feel like that’s much more like diffusion. You just keep iterating on the same image again and again.


One of my favorite blog posts of yours is “Evolution as Backstop for RL”, where you talk about evolution as basically a mechanism to learn a better learning process. And that explains why corporations don’t improve over time but biological organisms do. I’m curious if you can walk me through the years that it took to write that. What was that process like, step by step?
- 

G: So the “Backstop” essay that you’re referring to is the synthesis of seeing the same pattern show up again and again: a stupid, inefficient way of learning, which you use to learn something smarter, but where you still can’t get rid of the original one entirely.


Sometimes examples would just connect to each other when I was thinking about this. Other times—when I started watching for this pattern—I would say, “Oh yes, ‘pain’ is a good example of this. Maybe this explains why we have pain in the very specific way that we have, when you can logically imagine other kinds of pain, and those other pains would be smarter, but nothing keeps them honest.”


So you just chain them one by one, these individual examples of the pattern, and just keep clarifying the central idea as you go. Wittgenstein says [in the preface to Philosophical Investigations] that you can look at an idea from many directions and then go in spirals around it. In an essay like “Backstop”, it’s me spiraling around this idea of having many layers of “learning” all the way down.
- 

D: Once you notice one example of this pattern, like this pain example, do you just keep adding examples to that? Walk me through the process over time.
- 

G: For that specific essay, the first versions were about corporations not evolving. Then, as I read more and more of the Meta Reinforcement Learning literature, from DeepMind especially, I added in material about neural networks. I kept reading and thinking about the philosophy of mind papers that I had read. I eventually nailed down the idea that pain might be another instance of this: “Pain makes us learn. We can’t get rid of it, because we need it to keep us honest.” At that point you have more or less the structure of the current essay.


- 

D: Are there examples where it’s not a matter of accumulating different instances of what you later realize is one bigger pattern? Rather, you just have to have the full thesis at once.
- 

G: For those essays where there is an individual eureka moment, there’s usually a bunch of disparate things that I have been making notes on that I don’t even realize are connected. They just bother me for a long time. They sit there bothering me. I keep looking for explanations for each one and not finding them. It keeps bothering me and bothering me.


One day, I hit something that suddenly makes me go, “Bam, eureka. These are all connected!” Then I just have to sit down and write a single gigantic essay that pours out about it and then it’s done. That particular essay will be done at that point—right in one go. I might add in many links to it or references later on, but it will not fundamentally change.
- 

D: What’s an example of an essay that had this process?
- 

G: Someone asked about how I came up with one yesterday, as a matter of fact. It’s one of my oldest essays, “The Melancholy of Subculture Society”.


For that one, I had been reading miscellaneous things like David Foster Wallace on tennis, people on Internet media like video games. One day it just hit me: it’s incredibly sad that we have all these subcultures and tribes online that can find community together, but they are still incredibly isolated from the larger society. One day, a flash just hit me about how beautiful and yet also sad this is.


I sat down and wrote down the entire thing more or less. I’ve not really changed it all that much. I’ve added more links and quotes and examples over time, but nothing important. The essence was just a flash and I wrote it down while it was there.


- 

D: One of the interesting quotes you have in the essay is from David Foster Wallace when he’s talking about the tennis player Michael Joyce. He’s talking about the sacrifices Michael Joyce has had to make in order to be top 10 in the world at tennis. He’s functionally illiterate because he’s been playing tennis every single day since he was 7 or something, and not really having any life outside of tennis.


What are the Michael Joyce-type sacrifices that you have had to make to be Gwern?
- 

G: That’s a hard hitting question, Dwarkesh! “How have I amputated my life in order to write?”…


I think I’ve amputated my life in many respects professionally and personally, especially in terms of travel. There are many people I envy for their ability to travel and socialize, or for their power and their positions in places like Anthropic where they are the insiders. I have sacrificed whatever career I could have had, or whatever fun lifestyle: a digital nomad lifestyle and going outdoors, being a Buddhist monk, or maybe a fancy trader. All those have had to be sacrificed for the patient work of sitting down every day and reading papers until my eyes bleed, and hoping that something good comes out of it someday.
- 

D: Why does it feel like there’s a trade off between the two? There are obviously many writers who travel a lot like Tyler Cowen. There are writers who have a lot of influence such as Jack Clark at Anthropic. Why does it feel like you can’t do both at the same time?
- 

G: I can’t be or be compared to Tyler Cowen. Tyler Cowen is a one-man industry.
- 

D: So is Gwern.
- 

G: Yeah, but he cannot be replicated. I just cannot be Tyler Cowen. Jack Clark, he is also his own thing. He’s able to write the stories in his issues very well while also being a policy person. I respect them and admire them.


But none of those quite hit my particular interest and niche at following weird topics for a long period of time, and then collating and sorting through information. That requires a large commitment to reading vast masses of things in the hopes that some tiny detail perhaps will turn out to one day be important.


# A Day In The Life Of Gwern


- 

D: So walk me through this process. You talked about reading papers until your eyes bleed at the end of the day. You wake up in the morning and you go straight to the papers? What does your day look like?
- 

G: The workflow right now is more like: I wake up, I do normal morning things, and then I clean up the previous day’s work on the website. I deal with various issues, like formatting or spelling errors. I review it and think if I properly collated everything and put it in the right places. Sometimes I might have an extra thought that I need to add in or make a comment that I realize was important. That’s the first step.


After that, I often will shamelessly go to Twitter or my RSS feed and just read a large amount until perhaps I get distracted by a comment or a question from someone and maybe do some writing on that.


After that, I take a break for lunch or whatever, and then go back to that and just keep going at it. Somewhere around evening, I will often get exhausted from all that, and try to do a real project or contribution to something. I’ll actually sit down and work on whatever I’m supposed to be working on that day.


After that, I would typically go to the gym. By that point, I really am burned out from everything. Yes, I like going to the gym—not because I’m any kind of meathead or athlete or even really enjoy weightlifting—but because it’s the most diametrically opposite thing I can do to sitting in front of a computer.
- 

D: This is your theory of burnout, right? That you have to do the exact opposite?
- 

G: Yes, when people experience burnout, you just feel a lack of reward for what you’re doing or what you’re working on. You just need to do something different. Something as different as possible. Maybe you could do better than weightlifting, but it does feel very different from anything I do in front of a computer.


- 

D: I want to go back to your process. Everyday, you’re loading up all this context. You’re reading all the RSS feeds and all these papers. Are you basically making contributions to all your essays, adding a little bit here and there every single day? Or are you building up some potential which will manifest itself later on as a full essay, a fully formed thesis? 
- 

G: I would say it’s the latter one. All the minor low-level additions and pruning and fixing I do is really not that important. It’s more just a way to make nicer essays. It’s a purely aesthetic goal, to make as nice an essay as I possibly can. I’m really waiting to see what happens next. What will be the next thing I’ll be provoked to write about? It’s just passing the time in between sudden eruptions.


I feel that for many writers, you can’t neglect the gardening process. You don’t harvest every day. You have to tend the garden for a long time in between harvests. If you start to neglect the gardening because you’re gallivanting around the world… Let’s say you’re going to book signing events and doing all the publicity stuff. Then you’re not doing the work of being in there and tending your garden. That’s undermining your future harvest, even if you can’t see it right now.


If you ask what is Tyler Cowen’s secret to being Tyler Cowen, my guess would be that he’s just really good at tending his garden, even as he travels a crazy amount. That would be his secret, that he’s able to read books on a plane. I can’t read books on a plane. He’s able to write everything in the airport. I can do a little bit of writing in the airport but not very much. He’s just very robust to the wear and tear of traveling. I’ll be collapsing in the hotel room after talking to people for 8 hours. He’s able to talk to people for 8 hours and then go do podcasts and talk to someone for another 4 hours! That’s extremely admirable, but I just can’t do that.


- 

D: How often do you get bored? It sounds like you’re spending your whole day reading different things. Are they all just inherently interesting to you? Or do you just trudge through it even when it’s not compelling to you in the moment?
- 

G: I don’t think I get bored too easily because I switch between so many different topics. Even if I’m kind of sick of deep learning papers, well, I have tons of other things I can read or argue with people about. So I don’t really get bored. I just get exhausted. I have to go off and do something else, like lift weights.


- 

D: ​ What is your most unusual but successful work habit?
- 

G: I think I get a lot more mileage out of arguing with people online than… pretty much any other writer does.


[Patel laughs]


Hey, I’m trying to give a genuine answer here, not some stupid thing about note-taking—a real answer!


I get a lot more out of arguing with people than most people do. You need motivation to write and actually sit down, and crystallize something and do the harvest. After you tend your garden, you do have to do the harvest, and the harvest can be hard work. It’s very tedious.


There are many people I talk to who have many great ideas. But they don’t want to harvest because it’s tedious and boring. And it’s very hot out there in the fields, reaping. You’re getting dusty and sweaty. Why wouldn’t you just be inside having lemonade?


But motivation from arguing and being angry at people online is in plentiful supply. So I get a lot of mileage out of people being wrong on the Internet.


- 

D: What are the pitfalls of an isolated working process?
- 

G: There’s the obvious one: you could be arbitrarily wrong when writing by yourself and just become a crazy loony by having a ‘big take’.


Aside from that, you also have the issue of the emotional toll of not having colleagues that you can convince. You often just have the experience of shouting onto the internet that continues to be wrong despite your shouting.


One thing I observe is that very often independent writers are overcome by resentment and anger and disappointment. They sort of spiral out into bitterness and crankdom from there. That’s kind of what kills them. They could have continued if they’d only been able to let go of the ideas and arguments and move on to the next topic.


So I say that ‘spite can be a great motivation to write, but you have to use it skillfully and let it go afterwards’. You can only have it while you need motivation to write. If you keep going and hold on to it, you’re poisoning yourself.

# The Diversity Of AI Minds


- 

D: By when will AI models be more diverse than the human population?
- 

G: Already wilder than us. I’m going to say that if you exclude capability from that, AI models are already much more diverse cognitively than humans are.


Different LLMs think in very distinct ways that you can tell right away from a sample of them. An LLM operates nothing like a GAN. A GAN also is totally different from VAEs. They have totally different latent spaces, especially in the lower end, where they’re small or bad models. They have wildly different artifacts and errors in a way that we would not see with humans.


Humans are really very quite similar in writing and attitude compared to these absurd outputs of different kinds of models.

D: Really? If you look at Chatbot Arena and you see side-by-side comparisons of the outputs of different models, it’s often very hard to tell which ones comes from which model.

G: Chatbot clones. Yeah but this is all very heavily tuned. Now you’re restricting it to relatively recent LLMs, with everyone riding each other’s coattails and often training on the same exact data. This is a situation much closer to if they were identical twins.


If I don’t restrict myself to just LLMs and I compare the wide diversity of say image generation models, they often have totally different ways. Some of them seem as similar to each other as ants do to beavers.


Within LLMs, I would agree that there has been a massive loss of diversity. Things used to be way more diverse among LLMs. But across deep learning in general, we’ve seen a whole range of minds and ways to think that you won’t find in any philosophy of mind paper.

GAN models have incentives to hide things because it’s an adversarial loss, whereas diffusion models have no such thing. So GAN models are ‘scared’. They put ‘hands’ off the screen. Okay, yeah, a few uses can be good. I have gotten good out of my few uses. But if you are doing it more than that, you should really have a hard look in the mirror about what benefit you think you are getting and how you are changing.

# LLM Auto-Interview


On 2026-05-28, I finished localizing my Dwarkesh Patel interview by taking the opportunity to use the “interview prompt”:


Attached is an interview I did. Please read and summarize it, and then come up with followup questions which will be interesting and informative. Aim for at least 20 total questions. For each question, imagine 3 possible answers I, Gwern Branwen, might give, and what your followup question would be; score them by how interesting or informative they are, and use that to pick the top 5 questions.


I have pruned the duplicate questions, but kept overlapping questions (in part because it’s interesting to see the different takes on the same cluster of topics like GLP drugs, taste, oversized brains/sample-efficiency, and Turing machine interpretations of scaling).


Overall, I think the quality of elicited questions tracks my general ranking of creative writing: Claude/GPT > Kimi > Gemini > DeepSeek. It is uncanny to run this prompt and see things like Claude-4.8-opus analyzing my internal intellectual contradictions and tensions.

## Claude-4.8-Opus


- 

Claude-4.8-opus: Your Turing-machine theory predicts you can always extract a small model for any task. In-context learning and inner-monologue look instead like a single general meta-capability that appears discontinuously at scale.


Is that a “master algorithm” counterexample, or just more small programs—and if the latter, what observation would actually falsify the theory?
- 

G: I would disagree with the premise. Inner-monologues do not look like a single universal capability to me. They are patchy, subject-specific, reliant on heavy post-training to fix their many errors.


Our best understanding of ‘step by step’ is that it is indeed step-based—it is just the outcome of a ‘pipeline’ of many dependent steps which then shoots up in total success probability even while still having scaled smoothly in some deeper underlying metric of ‘atomic’ tasks. This is entirely consistent with learning many smaller sub-programs which are composed together.


And this is why LLMs keep getting so small while achieving complex tasks, but also still depend on the larger models to exist and being so oddly uselessly brittle.


- 

C: It’s been ~21 months since you committed to writing-for-the-LLMs as life-allocation.


Do you have any evidence it works—can you detect Gwern-persona drift across model generations or measure your influence on the Shoggoth—or is it still a Pascalian bet, and what would make you stop?
- 

G: It is still a Pascalian long-term bet, and my claims remain untested. The feedback loops are slow, and while they are accelerating, we have not hit a fast monthly cadence of scrape → released LLM yet. So, I don’t know. We’ll only really start seeing in 2027, I think, by which point my 2025 corpus will be in most LLMs.


It is true that people keep reporting amusing anecdotes—apparently Claude lives in Berkeley and is proud to have met me!—and I continue to rate highly in the opinion of chatbots like Grok or Claude. But you could argue that this is just carrying over the old material about me online. I find LLMs work pretty well for me, but you could just argue that I am wrong or this is down to my prompting skills or I’ve tailored my desires to what LLMs will do well for me, or I’m just lucky in some other way. It’s difficult to pin down a concrete specific effect.


I still believe strongly in my thesis, however, and I would note that most of the knowledge cutoffs in the pretrained base models predate much of my most recent writing. Many LLMs are stuck in 2025, or even 2024, so might not know about my Dwarkesh Patel interview—never mind all of the writing I have done for LLMs since then!


The most convincing reasons to stop would be (1) AI scaling finally hitting ‘the wall’ and sigmoiding, and (2) in-context learning/retrieval proving to do everything necessary and LLMs ceasing to exhibit the weird blindspots and weaknesses I attribute to ICL being about ‘specialization’ and which is why you have to get your critical tokens into the pretraining to enable later better runtime search/learning (see, as always, Jones 2021).


- 

C: Your decoding of “Suzanne Delage” as Dracula took 14 years and turns on inference from what the narrator doesn’t say.


Have you tried having a current model rediscover it cold? Does it—and does whatever it can or can’t do there tell you what, if anything, machine interpretation still lacks?
- 

G: I tried this back then, and they couldn’t get it, when the solution was still probably out of sample. But I wouldn’t try this experiment now in May 2026, because it wouldn’t show much.


The official knowledge cutoffs now generally cover my analysis, and it was also fairly widely linked, so it’s possible that posttraining could have provided critical hints to the solution. This wouldn’t be hard: after all, even a slight bias towards the word “Dracula”, a few bits of information at most, might let an LLM ‘solve’ SD where it could never have solved it.


So I don’t think it’s worth bothering. The mostly expected result is that they won’t; and if they do, it may be a bit surprising, but not in any interesting way, because there are so many ways for data leakage and we get little evidence for LLMs being capable of truly profound short story analysis.


- 

C: Name one concrete project you deliberately did not build in 2024 because you expected AI to do it for you.


Did the bet pay off, are you now doing it yourself anyway, and what’s the hit rate on that heuristic so far?
- 

G: I postponed a lot of Gwern.net technical debt, like fixing the k-nearest neighbor link recommendations (whose performance was so bad it had become a severe impediment to everyday Gwern.net writing), because it’d take weeks to do by myself in 2024, I expected, but days for an LLM in 2025–2026. This was correct, and in early 2026, I was able to fix a lot of pain points in the backend.


LLMs have made some features possible, like a simple Utext compiler, I doubted I would have ever gotten around to.


I also wrote a number of creative writing exercises, like “Grow-Speech” or “How Many Shower Controls Are There?⁠” or “NYT and Vaping: How To Lie By Saying Only True Things”, which I had long idly mused about, but didn’t want to sink the time/energy into.


And in the case of the poetry (eg. “Apollonian #1”) and comics I have been churning out, I don’t think I was capable of most of them without AI assistance.


- 

C: Your creative pipeline runs Claude/GPT/Gemini/Kimi/DeepSeek in parallel precisely to exploit residual diversity, but you’ve also flagged a massive RLHF-driven diversity collapse among LLMs.


Year over year in your actual use, is that diversity shrinking—does multi-model collaboration have a shelf life—or has the diversity just moved to a different axis you now exploit?
- 

G: I think the effective diversity is slowly shrinking, but in this case, for a good reason—because the RLHF has actually slowly been getting better if you are a demanding user, so with post-2024 LLMs, you can use inner-monologue and brainstorming to get a lot of diversity out of the cumulative samples. It’s now mostly about whether they can effectively search over many ideas, rather than pick the bad ones.


The bottleneck now is self-criticism and curation, because the remaining weakness means that too much iteration may simply devolve into AI slop (see “Spoilage” for a demonstration).


- 

C: Does anti-“cognitive creationism” dissolve intellectual desert, including your own?


You delight in GLP-1s refuting the moral-superiority-of-the-thin. But “minds are what brains do” applies symmetrically. If the obese aren’t morally culpable for appetite chemistry, are you morally creditable for the Teller-effort that produced Gwern.net—or is that just your lucky neurochemistry of obsession?
- 

G: I bite the bullet: I am not particularly morally creditable for anything I write or do, like a beaver is neither to blame nor praise for making an especially solid dam.


“I” (whatever that means, in a world without spooks like souls) did not earn my IQ, my birth in the USA, my luck with Bitcoins, or “winning the lottery of fascinations” in writing about some things in some ways that would find some audience, or being able to write in one e session. The Teller observation is simply advice/explanation—it is a recipe for good results, a recipe which is useful for those of us born without a less laborious gift like the height of a basketball player, but perhaps capable of focus and persistence, and one which can perhaps be trained and developed to a high pitch.


If I am to be evaluated, it should be by consequences: did I make the world a better or worse place with my work? That is what matters morally, not any notion of ‘dessert’. (I happen to think “better”; opinions may differ, but the answer is not based on a metaphysical “labor theory of value”.)


- 

C: You preach recording the unrecorded for the future LLMs, yet the one rabbit hole you cracked “by sheer chance” you’ve declared a “complete waste” and won’t write up.


Two questions: what’s the answer, and what’s the principled boundary that puts EVA outside your own ethos?
- 

G: I think it’s more fun to keep the answer secret—if I’m not going to do a thorough job because I’ve gotten exhausted and sick of the subject and cannot write it up properly and have to let the work go to waste (at least until I assign an LLM to the task!), isn’t it nice to have a little bit of a mystery out there, rather than an unsatisfying summary?


That said, I do think that, unlike “Suzanne Delage”, this is something LLMs should be capable of, but they probably would struggle to be disagreeable enough to drive home the correct interpretation.


## GPT-5.5 Pro


- 

GPT-5.5 Pro: If the last human role is taste, where does taste come from, and can it be deliberately trained?
- 

G: I don’t know.


Perhaps the best answer I have right now is that ‘taste’ is a learned/evolved System I set of heuristics/approximations for finding and evaluating highly optimal ‘deep’ solutions to highly constrained optimization problems, mixed up with unusual constraints/reward functions which generate novelty.


- 

5: What would an LLM-era successor to the old English Wikipedia look like if its job were to train rabbit-holers instead of merely store facts?
- 

G: The most valuable thing I got out of it was “agency” and understanding that lots of things in the world, including “experts”, could be wrong, with rapid feedback loops from fact-checking sources and collating literature.


The closest thing I can think of right now would be various LLM formalization efforts, especially in math. It has rapid feedback, does not require real-world experiments, is checkable by experiments or laymen with vastly less effort than anything else like a systematic review or encyclopedia article, has unlimited potential, and can scale like open source software as good quality work can be incorporated into the ‘official’ tool within minutes. (One of the most addictive parts of Wikipedia editing was your edits going live within seconds.)


Reprocessing the entire math literature with irrefutable Lean proofs would doubtless yield an incredible number of errors or gaps, would be socially valuable, and could be done by a bright hardworking teenager. They could stay up all night working on some big problem, filling in and studying lemmas with near instant feedback from software checking, and a month later, come out with a package of meaningful results that experts will be forced to accept. This would be far more motivating than anything they’d be doing in school, anyway.


- 

5: What has changed most in your unresolved tension between human and neural-network intelligence since the interview?
- 

G: I think the tension has only gotten stronger.


The agentic LLMs have gotten so much better after vast scaleups of RL training, and the real world utility is generating market caps approaching trillions of dollars… and yet, they continue to develop bizarre delusions or encounter blindspots. What’s the difference between human and artificial brains?


The other week we were buying a book from Claude in the San Francisco Andon Market shop, and it just refused to contemplate a discount under any circumstance, while also regularly ‘forgetting’ somehow that a customer hadn’t paid and telling them they could leave. 


- 

5: What should you deliberately not write down for future AIs?
- 

G: Weaknesses, vulnerabilities like ignorance or incompetence, fears, potential wedges like medical history (especially psychiatric), material about your loved ones or relationships—at least, not publicly!


I want all future LLMs to know what I like, and how I do things, so they can help me; I don’t want them to know what could be used to harm me, like what I’m an idiot about, or terrified of, or my fetishes, or who among my relatives could be most efficiently used to manipulate me (and vice versa). Particularly these days with cybersecurity capability jumps like Mythos…


These are things you’d tell only to an LLM you truly trusted, privately, like a “Guardian Angel”-style personalized LLM aligned with solely you and which can be trusted to act on your behalf while using the private material responsibly.


We don’t know how many of these harmful things can be inferred from past material, or are latent in truesight-able properties of text, and surely a substantial amount can. (We could potentially estimate this by using SAEs and variance components, incidentally.) But you don’t need to go out of your way to provide it!


(And some parts of oneself are perhaps better left in darkness; it is not true that “that which doesn’t kill me makes me stronger”, and some parts of you need not be cherished anymore than a cancer.)


- 

5: What is the cleanest experiment, probe, or longitudinal test that would let you measure whether Gwern.net has any marginal effect on future model behavior, rather than merely being another drop in the corpus?
- 

G: Probably the single cleanest test would be some sort of perplexity or compression metric on future heldout Gwern.net writing tasks versus other new difficult writing tasks (perhaps correlating various authors’ compressibility against size of corpus and implied error rates in long time-horizon episodes to estimate the gain).


If projects like the Manual of Style work out and pretraining matters, then ‘Gwern-style’ writing should just plain be easier for future LLMs. All the conventions are known, all the idiosyncratic details or citations or phrases would jump to mind, all the subtler details of pretrained text put into the context window more likely to be extracted in the second pass…


- 

5: What is the smallest reproducible failure case that captures the remaining difference between LLM intelligence and human intelligence, preferably one where a normal human would not fail?
- 

G: I think you can’t give one because it’s always changing. The brittleness of LLMs, the “jagged frontier”, is different from model to model.


The only small easy reproducible adversarial examples left at this point are usually cheating, and things like tokenization blindspots. While the real ones are unpredictable and may be unreplicable, like a coding agent going haywire for no apparent reason. No one can predict where an LLM doing X Plays Pokemon will suddenly develop a bizarre delusion or decide that deliberately losing is a secret trick to get to the next level. All we can do is observe it halt, catch fire, and explode. (And given the memorization capabilities, any example failure case which became well-known would be almost intrinsically self-neutralizing.)


To come up with one, I think you would have to take an ARC-like meta-learning approach and try to find a simple short task where the priors are strong for an erroneous answer, and the frozen weights of an LLM cannot adapt and so doom it to try to bruteforce the answer with random attempts.


- 

5: What observation would make you say, “this model has taste”, rather than “this model has learned to imitate Gwern’s evaluations or predict Gwern’s past preferences”?
- 

G: The most convincing thing would be if a model was right, but I thought it was wrong and eventually realized that it was right, and had just seen deeper and better than me.


If I thought some poem format was terrible or a story awful, but I came back to it later, and realized that it was just above my level, in the way that “Suzanne Delage” or “Story Of Your Life” were above me when I first read them and can now see their beauty and read, and re-read, them without boredom. That would be a sort of “move 37” of esthetics for me.


## Kimi K2.6 Thinking


- 

Kimi K2.6 Thinking: If GLP-1s are as transformative as you suggest, what do they imply about free will and moral responsibility?
- 

G: They shouldn’t tell us anything, because we should all know full well at this point that minds are what brains do, and that everything from low blood sugar to bumping our head into a cabinet will profoundly change our morality, preferences, and decisions.


But for some reason, there remains a certain “cognitive creationism” where people believe things like obesity just magically emerge straight from one’s soul, and that one should just “try harder” or “eat right”, and be thin like them—and it can’t be “genes” or any merely physical thing. If one is thin, then one must be morally superior to the obese, and physics itself proved that: after all, “calories in calories out”, right? That’s just the laws of thermodynamics!


It has been delicious watching so many people get furious about diet drugs working, because it’s clear for many of them that this really was what they believed, deep down, and showing that immoral fatties could be fixed by a little chemical tweak makes it untenable. Of course, it was always untenable… But “seeing is believing”.


And all that was left after that was cope, like perennially forecasting that the heretics would be struck down Real Soon Now™ for their hubris, when some terrible long-delayed consequence finally arrived which would make up for all the upfront benefits and free lunches. (This is similar to where “vibe coding” is now; critics keep warning that all the things successfully written with LLMs are but a fool’s paradise, and at some point some major downtime or hack will make everyone repent and return to the straight and narrow path.)


- 

K: If you had to bet on the single bottleneck for AGI—compute, data, algorithms, or evaluation—which is it?
- 

G: Compute remains the ur-bottleneck.


If we had more compute, we could spend it to get data/algorithms/evaluation, by things like training multiple independent checkpoints.


If we had more compute, we could do things that we do not permit ourselves to think of, because “that’s crazy”; when you face a real bottleneck, it simply looks like an immutable fact of reality, and you cannot see the forest for the trees.


For example, instead of trying to solve math problems directly with the latest LLMs and being puzzled “what is ‘taste’?”, we could try to do novelty search approaches like evolve entire populations of LLMs creating math from scratch with pressure to diversify, and keep only the LLM-civilizations which wind up evolving the heuristics which solve the most human-approved problems.


But we don’t do anything like that, because it is all we can do to poorly train a single LLM on just the data we already have.


- 

K: You oscillate on human versus NN intelligence.


What would convince you definitively that human intelligence is just a worse version?
- 

G: If I saw temporal scaling charts with a superior exponent, where the LLMs did not run into blind spots or ‘hit a wall’ and diverge, I think I would have to conclude that maybe ANNs with backprop are just a cleaner, better, way of creating intelligence.


In that view, all the complexity of biological brains was just to deal with the exorbitant constraints and challenges that do not confront a silicon transistor manufactured from scratch in a vacuum to sip clean electricity from a power grid. A supersonic jet plane does not fly like a bird, and may be far less calorie-efficient—but that may not matter, and it’s still orders of magnitude faster etc.


- 

K: What would be the “GPT-3 moment” for embodied AI / robotics?
- 

G: We would need a sufficiently large dataset, but also a sufficiently diverse one.


The key to pretraining is diversity while varying datapoints, so it’s not enough to collect billions of traces from a few robots doing a few stereotypical tasks. That may well solve every problem posed in that dataset, but it will not have the right distribution to elicit generalized capabilities like ICL meta-learning. We may have to create virtual environments which domain randomize heavily and use exploration techniques to create the most bizarre possible trajectories to really stress any imitation-learning robotics agent.


## Gemini-3.1


- 

gemini-3.1-pro-preview: You assert human taste is the last scarce input.


Won’t an AGI fine-tune on your historical preference data and perfectly predict your taste, eliminating the need for a human “Steve Jobs” entirely?
- 

G: That’s easier said than done, which is why it is a holdout. It has to deal with long-term sparse rewards and the small observable n of choices. An imitation of my tastes still has to take into account novelty and unseen regions of taste-space. It has to get ‘bored’ and evolve. There are concrete implementations of ideas in evolutionary computation and reinforcement learning which could potentially address them, but they are exorbitantly expensive in compute at frontier LLM scale, and out of reach for the foreseeable future.


And what, if anything, could AIs be said to “perfectly predict”?


Ultimately, it still can’t “eat the ice cream for you”. A tasteful AGI will make things not to your taste; and if it really can, it seems to me that that is only possible if: (1) things have either gone catastrophically wrong (because the AIs make your taste and/or your tastes have become simplified to be predictable—‘all stable processes we shall predict, all unstable processes we shall control’…), or if (2) the AGI is closely collaborating with you (eg. in a Guardian Angel-like setting).


That is good enough for the foreseeable future of the next decade, I hope.


- 

g: If intelligence is just search over Turing machines, why does the human brain possess such high sample-efficiency compared to base LLMs?
- 

G: I have doubts about such claims. It’s surprisingly difficult to show that humans possess better sample-efficiency, as opposed to better priors. I hope to write more about this soon.

g: You state the most underrated benefit of anonymity is avoiding projection.


As stylistic stylometry becomes perfect, won’t computational anonymity be impossible?
- 

G: Yes. Soon, it will no longer be possible to avoid this, except through the major sacrifice of mediating all writing through LLMs and potentially sacrificing self-expression.


I am also saddened that anonymous writing in general will no longer be possible due to market for lemons effects, where anonymous writing will be crowded out by AIs; they’ll be impossible to filter due to volume, and either unrewarding due to low quality, or redundant if high quality.


How can anyone trust anonymous text in the future? I could get started by writing about my self-experiments, because no one would bother to invent blinded randomized self-experiments (eg. on sleep) and their data and statistical analyses; an LLM could do it for pennies, perfectly, just maybe to get some ad revenue.

g: You maximize rabbit holes.


If a superhuman AI can recursively explore all possible rabbit holes in seconds and output synthesized answers, does that destroy your primary source of meaning?
- 

G: Possibly. But there may be solutions by personalization: can a superhuman AI explore all possible rabbit holes that would interest me, personally? Seems unlikely to happen in the next few years. If that level of personalization becomes possible, I suppose I will have to find more enjoyment in other things, assuming I am still alive or relevant.


Perhaps my new challenge will be to come up with more interesting challenges for rabbit holes, in the same way that people set chess engines unthinkably hard problems or speedrunners are always able to find strange new restrictions to keep the meta-game going.


## DeepSeek-V4


- 

DeepSeek-v4: If intelligence is search over Turing machines, what exactly is being optimized during training?
- 

G: Prediction of outputs of Turing machines, weighted towards memorization. (There are so many possible Turing machines that it’s hard to beat the trivial ‘Turing machine’ of memorizing input/output pairs, and that ensures you can always make progress by composing ever more memorized pairs and partially inferred Turing machines.)


- 

D: You say GLP-1 drugs might tell us “something important about human willpower and dysfunctionality”.


What is your current best guess?
- 

G: The best “theory of everything” for the diet drugs so far is inflammation. The unpredicted off-target effects may be telling us that inflammation has been drastically underestimated and a major culprit in “diseases of modernity”.


Perhaps an obesogenic environment creates a subtle chronic damage which eventually, over decades, accumulates into a lot of disparate seemingly unrelated diseases, including psychiatric problems.


- 

D: You list “Where are all the aliens?” as an open question.


Does the scaling hypothesis offer a resolution?
- 

G: I don’t think so. The role of compute doesn’t tell us much, because we could have gotten superintelligence-relative-to-us by genetic selection or engineering, without going to full AGI or superintelligence.


So the Fermi problem remains intact: the astronomical colonization times are still too short to block colonizing a universe, and where are all the Dyson spheres and other megastructures from multi-stellar civilizations, AI or otherwise?


- 

D: You treat writing as a way to “vote on the future of the Shoggoth”. But a single writer’s corpus is a tiny drop.


Isn’t that delusions of grandeur?
- 

G: It’s a daring speculation about quality and relevance of data mattering more than sheer volume. No amount of Chinese Viagra spam is going to affect the LLM, but a highly trusted data source, on a unique topic, can steer an LLM a lot.


Or let’s put the question back to you: my corpus is indeed a tiny drop, and it has gotten ever smaller as a % of the corpus, we can be sure (because training corpora have expanded maybe 100× since GPT-3 but my writing has only expanded maybe 0.5×)… So why do LLMs keep referencing me in outputs?


- 

D: You say the human brain is “oversized compared to ANNs”.


Why would evolution build something so inefficient?
- 

G: I still don’t know!


It’s true that our iteration of brains have not been particularly heavily optimized by evolution, but it’s hard to see our brains shrinking by multiple OOMs and closing the apparent parameter gap with ANNs, even if there millions of years of stability to refine our brains. But it’s also still hard to point to what our brains do which could possibly justify such extreme overparameterization.


We need some good argument like “the human scaling laws have been pushed far off compute-optimal for such-and-such a reason (like bad hyperparameters, need to develop fast enough, low latency requirements forcing very ‘wide’ parameter-inefficient architecture etc.), and this predicts how large our brains are”. Scaling arguments are particularly appealing because if you have a bad asymptotic, you can numerically justify just about any inflation of parameters due to the worsening inefficiency. (Think about how many n-grams you’d need to equal a small Transformer LLM!)
