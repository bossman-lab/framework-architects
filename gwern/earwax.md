While petting cats, I accidentally discovered about half of cats are fascinated by the smell & taste of earwax, particularly that of humans (both dry & wet), and this interest can last indefinitely. Dogs & humans, for comparison, are not. A number of anecdotes have reported this over the years, but no formal research appears to have been done on this.


What makes earwax attractive to cats? Pheromones? Some nutrient?


The best candidate to date is valeric acid, which is present in both human earwax & the cat attractant plant Valerian.


I accidentally discovered, by gently massaging cat ears while petting them, that most cats can enjoy fingers inserted into their ears—perhaps because, like humans, earwax can build up to uncomfortable levels (and I once discovered undiagnosed ear mites in a kitten this way).1 This was how, after testing about 7 cats, I then discovered that cats are moderately interested in the smell/taste of their own earwax; and some are almost obsessed. (I’m not entirely sure if they exhibit a Flehmen response, as Kolb 199135ya claims. Most of my tests occurred before I learned what a Flehmen response was.) This has held true of most cats I have tested this on. Dogs, on the other hand, usually don’t appreciate fingers in ears; and they especially show little interest in smelling or licking any kind of earwax.


Curious, I tested other easily-obtained earwaxes on cats, and I’ve found that cat earwax is not even the most interesting earwax: cats are more interested in dog earwax, and human earwax most of all: the reaction can be quite strong—given the opportunity, a cat will lick and chew a hearing aid for a while, and I’ve had to take away hearing aids from my cats because the intensity of their licking made me worry about them damaging it. Aside from the cost of replacement, this is a safety concern: hearing aids, earphones, or disposable foam earplugs are small enough to be eaten & endanger cats’ health.


Searching, I’ve found a number of comments in publications and online also noting this phenomenon. There are not many contexts a cat owner would notice cats’ interest in earwax, and many of them are actively discouraged (a cat licking your ear hurts!), but when there is, it appears that earwax interest is often noted.


I first thought that it might be like sniffing butts, a way to learn about the health/status of another cat2, but that doesn’t explain why dog & human earwax is more interesting, and cats don’t seem to seek out earwax even after they know about it (except for one of my cats who’d sometimes try to lick my ears when I was in bed). I find that I sometimes have to touch their noses with a waxy finger before they abruptly become interested (suggesting that whatever the odor is, it doesn’t travel far), and the earwax smell also appears to be fairly temporary as fresh earwax is much more interesting than old earwax, suggesting that the odor also is temporary and volatilizes away quickly (so it’s not a long-lasting chemical inside the earwax which requires eating).

# Sweat


I have also noticed that my cat was interested in my sweat, particularly after coming back from the gym. I had wiped off my forehead and humorously offered it to an overly-curious cat—and he was interested in licking it. This included sweat from my armpits, and to a lesser extent, my feet.


I was a little surprised because I hadn’t remembered my previous cat having any interest in sweat (despite her interest in my earwax).


In August 2025, I was fortunate enough to visit a woman with both a Savannah cat (ie. a serval hybrid) & an Egyptian mau domestic cat (a very rare but normal cat that looks like a small Savannah). Spontaneously, she warned me after taking off my leather coat & boots that, since I was a guy, the cats might be too interested in them This was a thing which happened regularly to men’s clothes, but never with any women’s’ clothing. Sure enough, both cats burrowed their heads into the boots and then the Savannah greatly enjoyed rubbing her head on the coat armpits.


When I tested them, the Egyptian mau cat had the normal domestic cat response to human earwax, but curiously, the Savannah cat was only mildly interested and quickly fled. Savannah cats can be extremely flighty, so it may be that the fear suppressed a normal response, as can happen with catnip, but it’s striking that it was the exotic hybrid which defied the trend.


There’s 3 possible implications of this anecdote: first, that the attractant chemical of my sweat is sex-specific and not present in female human sweat and could be found by looking at male-only chemicals (perhaps some testosterone byproduct?); second, that the sweat attractant is not the earwax attractant (because then the Savannah would respond to both—because why would the attractant be selectively secreted by my body?); and third, that the earwax response is specific to domestic cats and is missing in servals & perhaps arbitrarily many felids, and this has not been noticed before because no one makes a habit of testing out human earwax on random non-domestic cats.


# Chemistry


What is it about earwax that fascinates cats? Is there a particular chemical responsible, like a fat or salt or butyric acid?3 (While there is no shortage of confident assertions that the reason is the “incredibly high concentration of fatty acids and cholesterol” or salt in earwax and similar claims, zero evidence is ever offered for these explanations.)


Earwax in humans comes in ‘dry’ & ‘wet’ forms, differing by race and I would describe cat/dog earwax as being like ‘wet’ human earwax; do cats like ‘dry’ earwax as well? I didn’t run into any explicitly Asian or ‘dry’ anecdotes, so in 2024 I ran an online survey targeting East Asian cat-owners, and of the ‘dry’ participants who had noted any cat/earwax exposure, 26% (14⁄52) reported their cat was interested in the dry earwax (which was similar to the reported ‘wet’ rate), which would appear to strongly exclude the possibility of 0%, and also confirmed individual-differences in earwax response rate (≤50% of cats). “Identification of volatile organic compounds in human cerumen”, Prokop-Prigge et al 2014 notes that the VOCs do differ quite a bit by race-earwax type (typically less in East Asian)—butyric acid is common to both, but inasmuch as I didn’t see any result from my simple experiments, that may be irrelevant.


Prokop-Prigge et al 2014’s Table 1 lists the 46 major VOCs they found along with racial differences; some of them can probably be rejected out of hand as candidates: Limonene (no difference?) would presumably result in cats having a notable attraction to citrus fruits (they don’t); 2-butoxyethanol or acetic acid or 2-ethylhexanol (no difference?) are used in many cleaning/painting/food products, likewise for the almond-tasting benzaldehyde (difference), and given the lack of cat interest in human urine/feces, p-Cresol can probably be ignored despite attracting many other animals like mosquitoes or horses or bees. More intriguing is propionic acid (no difference), which WP describes as “body odor”-like and related to cheese (reminding one of cats & licking sweat), apparently often produced by gut bacteria (ie. smells like “guts of prey”?) and a fatty acid—immediately sounding relevant to feline dietary needs & preferences!—which can be used in animal feed but is not present in high levels in household products that cats would be exposed to regularly. Other VOCs listed of interest are: caproic acid, MSM, BHT, enanthic acid, & caprylic acid.

## Valerian


Most striking, however, is the entry of “pentanoic acid”, better known as valeric acid (no difference), as in, the second-most famous cat psychoactive, Valeriana officinalis! This would explain everything: the compound is not terribly common on its own, we know cats are attracted olfactorily to something in valerian (not necessarily the actinidine which is usually stated to be valerian extract’s active ingredient), cats are attracted to sweat too which contains isovaleric acid, valerian does not have any known effect on dogs and has minimal effect on humans (it’s used as a herbal remedy but in vastly larger quantities), valerian evaporates quickly, the Flehmen response is precisely how a cat responds to valerian, valerian response varies greatly between cats so that predicts earwax interest will vary (of the 2 cats I have tested with both valerian & earwax, the valerian responder was interested in earwax, and the non-responder was not), while the amount of pentanoic is inadequate to trigger any noticeable psychoactive effect and the valerian response tending to look like ‘passivity’ with the cat not seeking it out.


This explanation makes several predictions:

- 

purified valeric acid should be attractive
- 

dried or aged earwax should be low on valeric acid and low on attractiveness
- 

the ordering of valeric acid levels in earwax should be cat < dog < human
- 

East Asian earwax should be roughly as attractive (as Prokop-Prigge et al 2014 finds similar valeric acid levels in both dry & wet earwax) (which it turns out to be in the survey)
- 

there should be a high correlation between valerian-responding cats & earwax.4

# East Asian Survey


In August 2024, I ran an Internet survey on Prolific of n = 250 East Asian cat-owners asking about earwax type & if they had observed an earwax response from their cat. A majority had dry earwax, and most had not exposed their cat to earwax; of those who did, a majority observed no response; and of responders, 26% of dry earwax owners noted a response vs 43% of wet. This confirms that cats have individual differences in earwax response, and that dry earwax can trigger a weaker response—consistent with the valeric acid theory.


After reading all the available anecdotes, one of the biggest questions I had was whether the earwax effect is exclusive to ‘wet’ human earwax, which is the most common type in the West where all these anecdotes are coming from; no one specified being East Asian or having the other ‘dry’ earwax type. If there was no earwax effect for the dry type, that would be important to know for the chemical implications (and as evidence against the valeric acid theory).


I would have used Google Surveys to run a translated survey in Japanese, but Google shut it down. 3 years later in August 2024, it annoyed me I had still not run into anything on this topic, and I asked on Twitter about East Asian survey options and someone suggested Prolific; I was familiar with Prolific from its frequent use in psychology studies as an upgrade over Amazon Mechanical Turk (which appears to be suffering from increasing bot/fraud/quality issues) and Spencer Greenberg’s experiments/writings, but I had never gotten around to trying it myself. This was as good a reason as any.


I signed up and was surprised to see that Prolific allowed screening by not just ethnicity but pet ownership as well, including cats. So I could easily run a cheap ($50) survey of just n = 250 East Asian cat-owners, and then after the loss from people not having exposed cats to earwax or having the wrong wet earwax, I would at least be able to confirm or rule out an earwax effect of 0%. (Which would correspond to dry earwax lacking the critical chemical; if it contained the secret ingredient X, then a priori we would expect the effect to be weaker, because the dry earwax has fewer volatiles, and so people would be more likely to see little or no effect from measurement error.)

## Survey Results


Given the screening, I worded it as a simple 2-question multiple-choice survey (CSV results):


- 

Is your earwax ‘wet’ (brown/sticky) or ‘dry’ (grey-tan/brittle)?

- 

wet [n = 109]
- 

dry [n = 144]

- 

Has your cat ever been exposed to your earwax; and was interested in it?

- 

No; N/A [n = 153]
- 

Yes; No [n = 65]
- 

Yes; Yes [n = 35]


As expected, most results were not useful, but enough were to answer the question: dry earwax reported 14 responses vs 38 non-responses or 26%, and wet 21 vs 27 or 43%.5 Thus, we can strongly rule out 0%: dry earwax does trigger an earwax effect.


An interesting secondary implication here is that earwax effect is not universal: it seems many cats—perhaps even the majority—do not show any interest in human earwax. (This trend was visible in the anecdotes, but this sample size is much larger and confirms it.)


And if we take the overall average rate of 40%, we get a rate which looks like you would expect for the cat psychoactives like catnip or valerian. For example, a rate of 40% is consistent with the best estimate of valerian response rate, 50% (from the experimental data of Bol et al 2017), but biased downwards by measurement error (from issues like owners having only a little bit of earwax at a time).


The logical next question is then: is there a correlation between valerian and earwax?


Unfortunately, valerian root extract is used so rarely by cat owners that surveys are unlikely to dig up enough cat-owners who have already experimented with both earwax and valerian, and would start to run into methodological issues (eg. most Internet survey responses on that topic claiming to be that extremely unusual cat-owner might wind up being liars).


So this would likely require experimental investigation similar to Bol et al 2017 of a large group of cats in-person, where one can administer both valerian & earwax exposures.

Butyric acid is, in addition to being known for its distinctive smell and presence in many substances like butter or animal fat or milk, is implicated in countless biological processes, and is a logical candidate for the earwax active ingredient. Both butyric acid and salt would also explain the interest in licking sweaty hands/armpits.


I tried to crudely test out the possibility of butyrate on my earwax-loving cat. A 200620ya chemistry forum discussion outlined a number of strategies for obtaining butyric acid, of which the second simplest was adding lye and the simplest was just rancid butter.


Since I had no lye lying around, I made rancid butter by putting a few tablespoons of butter into a ziplock bag inside a tupperware and storing it on top of my hot water heater for a week and a half; after a day it had melted and separated into a yellow liquid & a solid white mass, and after a week it smelled bad. I stored it in my refrigerator.


I tested my cat 3× by paired dish comparisons (similar to previous tests of water or food): offering 2 saucers of a tablespoon of rancid butter and butter which I heated simultaneously in my microwave for 10s. All 3 times, if he started with the rancid butter, he would eventually lick and eat a little of the rancid butter, but slowly and without enthusiasm, and switch to the fresh butter which he would eat more of and much more enthusiastically, and vice versa, always leaving more of the rancid than fresh. The earwax-style fascination was absent for both kinds of butter. It’s possible that the rancid butter had other breakdown products which offset the appeal of the butyric acid, but I would have expected different behavior if he was simultaneously attracted & repelled.


I further tested ‘caramelized butter’ (butter heated up to a point where it browns, which can be sweetened and used as delicious cake frosting); my cat liked it, but another cat I tried was almost indifferent.


For a followup experiment, I bought some lye and mixed 2tsp lye with a quarter stick of melted butter (vs another quarter, both left to cool to room temperature), which turned it darker yellow and gave it an odd smell (albeit not nearly as strong as the ‘rotten’ butter smelt), which eventually hardened to a cracked white crust, and tried it on my cat; again, he showed no interest in the treated butter compared to the normal ‘fresh’ butter.↩︎
